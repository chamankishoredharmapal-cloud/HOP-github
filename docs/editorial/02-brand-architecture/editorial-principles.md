# Editorial Principles

These six principles govern every editorial decision at House of Padmavati. They apply to every content type, every page, every sentence. If a piece of writing violates a principle, it does not publish.

## 1. Show, Don't Tell

**Principle:** Sensory language carries more weight than abstract praise. Instead of telling the reader that something is beautiful, show them what it looks like, feels like, weighs like, moves like.

**Why:** Abstract praise ("stunning," "exquisite," "gorgeous") triggers no sensory response. Specific description triggers mirror neurons — the reader mentally simulates the experience of touching, wearing, and owning the saree. This is the mechanism of virtual possession, which activates the endowment effect and elevates perceived value.

**Enforcement:** Every content unit must contain at least one sentence per sense relevant to the content (sight, touch, weight). If the word "beautiful" or "stunning" appears, the sentence must be rewritten.

| Instead of | Write |
|------------|-------|
| "A beautiful silk saree in a stunning shade of green." | "A Mulberry silk that reads as teal in shadow and jade in sunlight — the weft catching each shift." |
| "Exquisite craftsmanship." | "Twenty-one days on the loom. The weaver adjusts the tension twice an hour." |
| "A gorgeous drape." | "The fabric settles at the hip with a weight that says 'I am here.'" |

## 2. Specific Over Generic

**Principle:** Specificity is the currency of trust. Generic language is the currency of doubt. Every detail that can be specific must be specific.

**Why:** Specificity signals authenticity. When a brand says "Pondicherry, 5:47 AM" instead of "morning," the reader's brain registers that this is a real place, a real time, a real observation. Generic language signals marketing — words chosen for effect, not truth.

**Enforcement:** Every claim must pass the "provenance test" — can this claim be traced to a specific place, person, technique, or moment? If not, it must be made specific or removed.

| Instead of | Write |
|------------|-------|
| "Our sarees are made by skilled artisans." | "Woven by Gangamma in Molakalmuru, on a pit loom her grandmother used." |
| "Premium quality fabric." | "Mulberry silk, 12-momme, hand-dyed in small batches." |
| "A special occasion saree." | "This saree belongs to winter weddings where the light fades by five." |

## 3. Rooted in the Real

**Principle:** Every claim should be traceable to a real weaver, a real place, a real technique, or a real moment. HOP does not embellish or exaggerate. The truth of handwoven cloth is remarkable enough.

**Why:** The premium saree market is saturated with exaggerated claims. Buyers have learned to be sceptical. Rooting every claim in the real is the most powerful differentiator HOP has. It also satisfies System 2 — the rational mind that needs proof before committing to a high-involvement purchase.

**Enforcement:** Every product description must include at least one verifiable fact: the weave type, the fabric origin, the weaver's location, the technique name. Every collection must name its region. Every journal article must identify its place and time.

| Dimension | Real | Unreal |
|-----------|------|--------|
| **Weaver** | Named or identified by community | "Our artisans" |
| **Place** | Specific village or town | "India" (too broad for a craft-specific brand) |
| **Time** | Specific hour, season, or year | "Recently" |
| **Technique** | Named weave or process | "Traditional methods" |
| **Material** | Specific fiber, weight, origin | "Premium fabric" |

## 4. Restrained

**Principle:** The most powerful word is the one you choose not to use. Restraint is not emptiness — it is the removal of everything that does not serve the cloth, the woman, or the story.

**Why:** Luxury is signalled not by abundance but by curation. A page with one beautiful image and ten words of copy feels more luxurious than a page with a gallery, three CTAs, five trust badges, and a testimonial carousel. Restraint signals confidence. Clutter signals anxiety.

**Enforcement:** Every page must be reviewed for verbal and visual clutter. If an element can be removed without diminishing the experience, remove it. If a sentence can be cut without losing meaning, cut it.

| Apply restraint to | Guideline |
|--------------------|-----------|
| Adjectives | Use one precise adjective instead of two generic ones. |
| CTAs | One primary CTA per page. No secondary CTAs that compete. |
| Trust signals | Show them on demand (accordion), not by default. |
| Product details | Lead with the story. Technical details are available but never forced. |
| Journal articles | No "you might also like" at the bottom. Let the reader sit with what they just read. |

## 5. Human-Scale

**Principle:** HOP tells stories of individual women — not demographics, segments, or target audiences. Every piece of content should feel like it was written for one person, not a million.

**Why:** The premium saree purchase is deeply personal. It is tied to identity, family, memory, and aspiration. Abstract marketing language ("our customer," "the modern woman") cannot reach this depth. Personal, specific language can.

**Enforcement:** Write as if addressing one woman. Use "you" and "your." When describing the wearer, use "she" and "her" — a specific woman, not an archetype. Every story should include at least one human detail: a name, a relationship, a memory, a preference.

| Abstract | Human-Scale |
|----------|-------------|
| "The modern working woman needs a saree that transitions from office to evening." | "She wears it to the studio, then to dinner — and it carries both with equal ease." |
| "Our customers value authenticity." | "She chose this weave because her mother wore one like it." |
| "Perfect for weddings." | "She will wear this to her niece's wedding — the one where the whole family gathers after years." |

## 6. Trust Through Transparency

**Principle:** Trust is not claimed. It is built through visible, verifiable transparency. Show the reader how the saree is made, where it comes from, who made it, and how to care for it. Hide nothing.

**Why:** The loss aversion bias makes premium buyers highly sensitive to risk. Every hidden detail is a potential reason to abandon the purchase. Every revealed detail is a reason to trust. Transparency is the most effective risk-reduction strategy available to a direct-to-consumer brand.

**Enforcement:** Every product page must have System 2 content available on demand (accordion or similar) covering: weave specifications, fabric composition, care instructions, origin, and dispatch information. Every policy page must be written in plain language, not legalese. Every journal article about craft must name its sources.

| Visible | Hidden |
|---------|--------|
| Weave type, fabric, origin | "Premium materials" |
| Care instructions (specific) | "Dry clean only" |
| Dispatch estimate | "Ships in 5-7 business days" without explaining why |
| Weaver region | "Handmade in India" |
| Pricing (all-inclusive) | Hidden fees at checkout |
