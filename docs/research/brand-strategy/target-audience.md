# Target Audience

## Source Attribution

- **Origin:** Derived from the Brand Bible audience definition, customer feedback, and behavioral economics analysis from the Premium Saree Strategy Playbook.
- **Date:** July 2026
- **Related documents:** persona-primary.md, persona-secondary.md, persona-tertiary.md, positioning.md

## Executive Summary

HOP serves three audience tiers, each with distinct needs, values, and content requirements. The primary audience is a discerning Indian woman who values craft over fashion and legacy over trends. The secondary audience is the intentional gift giver. The tertiary audience is the curious visitor who discovers HOP through content and may convert over time. All three are reached through the same editorial voice but with different content strategies.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: Values-Based Segmentation

Traditional luxury segmentation uses income and geography. HOP segments by values and relationship to craft. The primary audience is defined not by what she earns but by how she chooses — she buys less but chooses better, she reads before she purchases, she values authenticity over branding.

**Editorial implication:** Content must speak to values, not demographics. Do not write for "women aged 28-55, income bracket X." Write for "the woman who has learned to recognize quality by touch."

**Example:** A journal article is not "for working women" — it is for "the woman who wears her saree to the studio because it is the most herself she feels."

### Principle 2: Life-Stage Targeting

The primary audience's relationship with sarees evolves across life stages:
- **Early career (28-35):** Building a wardrobe of quality pieces, learning about craft, attending peers' weddings
- **Mid-career (35-45):** Established taste, buying for weddings and milestones, beginning to think about legacy
- **Established (45-55):** Deep knowledge of craft, buying for daughters and daughters-in-law, investing in heirlooms

**Editorial implication:** Content should meet each life stage — craft education for the early stage, milestone occasion guides for the mid stage, generational framing for the established stage.

**Example:** A craft note on zari serves the early-stage learner. A collection narrative for a wedding collection serves the mid-stage buyer. A house letter on continuance serves the established buyer.

### Principle 3: Psychographic Over Demographic

Demographics (age, income, location) are weak predictors of premium saree purchase behavior. Psychographics (values, attitudes, interests) are strong predictors. The primary buyer in Mumbai and the primary buyer in the US diaspora share values even though their demographics differ.

**Editorial implication:** Content strategy is driven by psychographic segments (discerning buyers, gift givers, curious visitors), not by geographic or income-based segments.

**Example:** A product story uses the same sensory language for an India-based buyer and a US diaspora buyer — the values (craft, authenticity, intention) transcend location.

## Application to HOP

| Audience | Content Approach | Primary Pages | Key Values |
|----------|-----------------|---------------|------------|
| **Primary** (discerning woman) | Deep editorial, craft education, sensory writing | Journal, Collections, Product Detail | Authenticity, craft, legacy, quietness |
| **Secondary** (gift giver) | Intention, ceremony, presentation | Gift, Product Detail, Checkout | Meaning, discretion, beauty |
| **Tertiary** (curious visitor) | Discovery, education, visual beauty | Journal, Homepage, About | Learning, beauty, discovery |
