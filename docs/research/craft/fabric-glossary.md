# Fabric Glossary

## Source Attribution

This glossary synthesises information from *Textile Fabrics: A Handbook* by S. Muthu, *Silk: Processing, Properties and Applications* by K. Murugesh Babu, *The Textile Book* by Colin Gale and Jasbir Kaur, technical literature from the Central Silk Board of India, and handloom cluster research reports. Sensory descriptions have been validated through handling of fabric samples.

## Executive Summary

Fabric knowledge is the foundation of editorial authority. Each fibre type behaves differently — in drape, breathability, ageing, care. Using precise fabric vocabulary in product copy and editorial content signals expertise and helps customers make informed choices. Sensory language about fabric creates virtual possession: a reader who can imagine how a saree feels is a reader who will buy.

## Key Principles

### Editorial Principle: Sensory Language About Fabric Creates Virtual Possession

Describing how a fabric feels against skin, how it catches light, how it moves, allows a reader to mentally possess the garment before purchase. This is particularly important for online saree sales, where touch is impossible.

## Fabric Descriptions

### Mulberry Silk (*Bombyx mori*)

- **Source:** Cultivated silkworms fed exclusively on mulberry leaves.
- **Properties:** Long, continuous filament. High natural sheen. Even texture. Strongest of the commercial silks. Takes dye evenly.
- **Feel:** Smooth, cool, almost fluid. Drapes with a liquid quality. Weight ranges from gossamer (4–6 momme) to heavy crepe (16+ momme).
- **Care:** Dry clean recommended. Can be gently hand-washed in cold water with mild detergent. Do not wring. Iron on low heat while slightly damp.
- **Editorial note:** The term "mulberry" adds specificity — distinguish from "silk" generically. A saree described as "mulberry silk" is immediately positioned above an unnamed silk.

### Tussar Silk (*Antheraea mylitta*)

- **Source:** Wild silkworms feeding on naturally growing trees (sal, arjun, oak).
- **Properties:** Shorter, coarser filament. Natural pale gold or copper colour. Irregular texture — nubby, slubbed. Does not take chemical dyes easily (natural dyes adhere better).
- **Feel:** Warm, textured, matte. Drapes with more structure than mulberry — holds shape. Ages well, softens with wear.
- **Care:** Dry clean recommended. Avoid prolonged sun exposure (natural colour fades beautifully but unevenly).
- **Editorial note:** Tussar's irregularities are features, not flaws. Name them. "The natural slub gives each saree a surface that catches light in unexpected places."

### Cotton

- **Source:** Cellulose fibre from the cotton plant (*Gossypium*).
- **Properties:** Breathable, absorbent, hypoallergenic. Varies enormously in quality by staple length and weave density.
- **Feel:** Cool against skin, soft, relaxed. Udupi cotton is famously fine — almost translucent when held to light. Heavier handloom cottons have a crispness that softens with washing.
- **Care:** Machine or hand wash in cold water. Colour separation for first washes. Iron on high heat. Improves with age.
- **Editorial note:** Specify the cotton type (Udupi, Bengal, organically grown). "Karnataka cotton" or "handloom cotton" adds specificity.

### Linen

- **Source:** Flax plant (*Linum usitatissimum*) stem fibres.
- **Properties:** Stronger than cotton. High absorbency. Low elasticity — wrinkles characteristically. Conducts heat well, feels cool.
- **Feel:** Crisp, textured, substantial. Softens dramatically with washing. The wrinkles are part of the material's character — a sign that it is real linen, not a blend.
- **Care:** Hand or machine wash in warm water. Iron while very damp. Linen "blooms" with use.
- **Editorial note:** Do not apologise for linen's wrinkles. Frame them as the fabric's honest expression.

### Organza

- **Source:** Silk (or synthetic) woven in a plain weave with high twist yarns.
- **Properties:** Stiff, transparent, lightweight. Holds structure — does not drape softly. Traditionally used for veils, overlays, and saree borders.
- **Feel:** Crisp, almost papery. Rustles when moved. Sheer enough to reveal the fabric beneath.
- **Care:** Dry clean only. Iron on low heat with a pressing cloth. Frays easily — handle with care.
- **Editorial note:** Organza is a specialist fabric. Use it to demonstrate technical knowledge — "the organza border is woven with a higher twist count than the body, which gives it that distinct stiffness."

### Blends

- **Silk-cotton:** Combines silk's sheen with cotton's breathability. Medium weight. Suitable for humid climates. Drapes better than pure cotton, breathes better than pure silk.
- **Silk-wool:** Warm, substantial, textured. Rare in Indian handloom but increasingly used in winter-weight sarees.
