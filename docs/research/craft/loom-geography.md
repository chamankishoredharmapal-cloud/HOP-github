# Loom Geography: Weaving Clusters of Karnataka and Tamil Nadu

## Source Attribution

This document is based on the Handloom Census 2019 (Ministry of Textiles), *Handloom Clusters of South India* (Development Commissioner of Handlooms), *Textile Geography of India* by P. N. Mehta, district-level handloom reports from the Karnataka and Tamil Nadu State Handloom Directorates, and field surveys conducted in weaving communities. Population figures for active weaver families are estimates from the most recent census data.

## Executive Summary

India's handloom sector is organised into geographic clusters — villages and towns where specific weaving communities have concentrated for generations. Each cluster has developed a distinct specialisation shaped by local materials, climate, historical trade routes, and community knowledge. For HOP's editorial voice, weaving geography is not background detail — it is core content. Naming a place situates the saree in a real landscape and a real community.

## Key Principles

### Editorial Principle: Place Names Add Specificity and Authenticity

"Mysore silk" is not a generic term — it refers to silk woven in a specific region with specific water quality, climate conditions, and community knowledge. The place is part of the product's identity.

## Karnataka

### Ilkal (Bagalkot district, North Karnataka)

- **Known for:** Tope teni pallu sarees, cotton and silk combinations, temple borders.
- **Weaver community:** Primarily the Devanga community.
- **Active looms (estimated):** ~1,500–2,000.
- **Key characteristics:** Distinctive interlocking pallu technique. Red and white traditional colour palette. Cotton body with silk border is common.
- **Access:** Approximately 450km from Bengaluru. Connected by road and rail to Bagalkot.

### Molakalmuru (Chitradurga district, Central Karnataka)

- **Known for:** Elaborate gopuram (temple spire) borders on jacquard, pure silk sarees.
- **Weaver community:** Primarily the Neygi community. Fewer than 200 active families remain.
- **Active looms (estimated):** ~800–1,000.
- **Key characteristics:** Wide, ornate borders. Real zari work. Fine silk body. The cluster is in decline — younger weavers are leaving the trade.
- **Access:** Approximately 250km from Bengaluru. Small town with limited infrastructure.

### Mysore (Mysuru district, South Karnataka)

- **Known for:** Pure mulberry silk sarees, lightweight, matte finish. Mysore silk GI tag.
- **Weaver community:** Various, including the Karnataka Silk Industries Corporation (KSIC) and independent weavers.
- **Active looms (estimated):** ~500 (excluding KSIC industrial looms).
- **Key characteristics:** Fine, plain silk. Minimal borders. Pastels and jewel tones. GI-tagged production is regulated.
- **Access:** Major city, well-connected.

### Udupi (Udupi district, Coastal Karnataka)

- **Known for:** Extra-fine handwoven cotton sarees, lightweight and breathable.
- **Weaver community:** Primarily the Devanga community.
- **Active looms (estimated):** ~600–800.
- **Key characteristics:** Fine cotton, checked or striped patterns. Known for comfort in humid coastal climate.
- **Access:** Coastal town, accessible via Mangalore.

### Gajendragad (Gadag district, North Karnataka)

- **Known for:** Patteda Anchu (angled border) sarees.
- **Weaver community:** Small, concentrated community.
- **Active looms (estimated):** ~200–300.
- **Key characteristics:** Distinctive angled border. Cotton and silk-cotton blends. Minimalist aesthetic.
- **Access:** Small town in North Karnataka, less visited.

## Tamil Nadu

### Kanchipuram (Kanchipuram district)

- **Known for:** Heavy pure silk sarees with wide zari borders. India's most famous silk saree cluster.
- **Weaver community:** Primarily the Saliya and Devanga communities.
- **Active looms (estimated):** ~5,000–6,000 (declining from ~15,000 in the 1990s).
- **Key characteristics:** Three-ply or two-ply silk, heavy weight, contrasting borders, elaborate zari work. The silk is distinctive for its durability and gloss.
- **Access:** Major town, ~75km from Chennai. Well-connected by road and rail.

### Chettinad (Sivaganga district)

- **Known for:** Cotton sarees with bold block-printed patterns and temple borders. Also known for chettinad cotton checks.
- **Weaver community:** Primarily the Chettiar community (patronage) and local weavers.
- **Active looms (estimated):** ~300–500.
- **Key characteristics:** Distinctive printed patterns, stiff cotton that softens with washing. Less known than Kanchipuram but with a dedicated following.
- **Access:** Remote, approximately 90km from Madurai.

### Madurai (Madurai district)

- **Known for:** Cotton and silk sarees with temple borders, known as Madurai cotton or Madurai silk.
- **Weaver community:** Various communities.
- **Active looms (estimated):** ~1,000–1,500.
- **Key characteristics:** Known for sungudi (tie-dye) and temple border sarees. The cotton is medium-weight and durable.
- **Access:** Major city, well-connected.

### Salem (Salem district)

- **Known for:** Pure silk sarees, medium weight, known for vegetable-dyed silk. Salem silk is less glossy than Kanchipuram but more matte and subtle.
- **Weaver community:** Devanga and other communities.
- **Active looms (estimated):** ~2,000.
- **Key characteristics:** Known for natural dye use. Medium weight. Often more affordable than Kanchipuram.
- **Access:** Major city, well-connected.

## Notable Clusters Beyond Karnataka and Tamil Nadu

- **Chanderi (Madhya Pradesh):** Fine silk-cotton sarees, known for transparency and lightweight texture.
- **Maheshwar (Madhya Pradesh):** Cotton and silk sarees with distinctive border designs and zari work.
- **Varanasi (Uttar Pradesh):** Heavy silk sarees with intricate brocade work (Banarasi).
- **Baluchari (West Bengal):** Narrative silk sarees with mythological scenes woven into the border.
- **Sambalpuri (Odisha):** Ikat technique, distinctive geometric patterns.
