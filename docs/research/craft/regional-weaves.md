# Regional Weaves of Karnataka and Tamil Nadu

## Source Attribution

This document draws from *Handloom Clusters of Karnataka* (Development Commissioner of Handlooms), *The Sarees of India* by Rta Kapur Chishti, *Handwoven Textiles of South India* by Sesha Jyoti, field research and weaver interviews conducted in Ilkal, Molakalmuru, and Kanchipuram, and the Handloom Census 2019 by the Ministry of Textiles. First-hand observations from cluster visits supplement published sources.

## Executive Summary

Each weaving cluster in South India has a distinct identity defined by geography, community, available raw materials, and historical market. The weave, the border pattern, the pallu design, even the way a saree is folded and sold — all differ by region. For HOP's editorial voice, naming the place is an act of authentication. "Ilkal saree" carries centuries of meaning that "handwoven saree" cannot.

## Key Principles

### Editorial Principle: Place Names Add Specificity and Authenticity

A saree described as "from Ilkal" immediately connects the garment to a real place, a real community, a real tradition. Place names are trust markers. They also carry responsibility — getting the details right matters.

## Regional Weave Profiles

### Ilkal (Bagalkot district, Karnataka)

- **Distinctive feature:** The *tope teni* pallu — the border is woven separately and joined to the body using a distinctive interlocking technique at the pallu edge. The border runs along both edges of the pallu but does not continue down the body.
- **Borders:** Temple motifs, parrots, rudraksha, geometric patterns. Traditionally in red, maroon, and white.
- **Body:** Often in a contrasting colour, lighter weight, simpler weave.
- **Material:** Cotton body with silk border, or fully silk.
- **Editorial note:** The *tope teni* technique is one of the most distinctive in Indian handloom. It is also expensive — the interlocking requires a separate weaver. Mention this in product copy to justify pricing.

### Molakalmuru (Chitradurga district, Karnataka)

- **Distinctive feature:** Elaborate temple border (gopuram) woven on a jacquard attachment. The gopuram pattern is the regional signature — tiered temple spires with intricate geometric fill.
- **Borders:** Gopuram, elephants, floral vines. Wide and heavy, often with zari.
- **Body:** Fine silk, plain or with minimal pattern. The border is the event.
- **Material:** Pure silk, often with real zari border.
- **Editorial note:** Molakalmuru weaving is a specialised skill — fewer than 200 families remain active. This scarcity is not manufactured; it is genuine. Write with awareness of the craft's fragility.

### Patteda Anchu (Karnataka, specific to Gajendragad and surrounding areas)

- **Distinctive feature:** Angled border — the border pattern runs at an angle rather than parallel to the edge. The name means "angled border" in Kannada.
- **Borders:** Simple geometric angled bands, often in contrasting colours. Minimalist compared to Molakalmuru.
- **Body:** Cotton or silk-cotton blend, lightweight.
- **Material:** Predominantly cotton, occasionally silk.
- **Editorial note:** The angled border is visually subtle but technically demanding. It is a quiet craft detail that appeals to connoisseurs.

### Mysore Silk (Mysore region, Karnataka)

- **Distinctive feature:** Pure mulberry silk, extremely fine, lightweight, with a matte finish (distinct from Kanchipuram's glossy finish). Known for the *Mysore silk* GI tag.
- **Borders:** Minimal — thin contrast border, often without zari. The fabric is the statement.
- **Body:** Plain or with subtle checks. No heavy embroidery.
- **Material:** Pure mulberry silk, often in pastels and jewel tones.
- **Editorial note:** Mysore silk is the closest Indian saree equivalent to Loro Piana's philosophy — the material is the message. Let the product copy reflect this restraint.

### Udupi Cotton (Udupi district, Karnataka)

- **Distinctive feature:** Extra-fine cotton, almost translucent, incredibly soft. Lightweight and breathable. Known as the "air-conditioned" cotton.
- **Borders:** Simple — thin coloured border, occasionally with a narrow zari line.
- **Body:** Fine checked or striped patterns, or solid colours.
- **Material:** Pure cotton, hand-spun and handwoven.
- **Editorial note:** Udupi cotton is an everyday luxury. The editorial tone should be warm and accessible — this is the saree you wear at home, not just for occasions.

### Kanchipuram (Kanchipuram district, Tamil Nadu)

- **Distinctive feature:** Heavy pure silk, wide contrasting borders with elaborate zari work, distinctive pallu designs. The silk is traditionally three-ply or two-ply, giving it weight and durability.
- **Borders:** Wide, with temple motifs, floral patterns, peacocks, and geometric designs. Heavy zari borders are standard.
- **Body:** Contrast colour, often with small checks or stripes (temper or kattam patterns).
- **Material:** Pure mulberry silk with zari (often silver coated with gold).
- **Editorial note:** Kanchipuram is India's most famous silk saree. HOP's approach should differentiate — focus on specific weaver communities, specific zari types, specific border motifs. The general "Kanchipuram silk" market is crowded; specificity is the escape route.

### Chettinad (Chettinad region, Tamil Nadu)

- **Distinctive feature:** Known for cotton sarees with bold block-printed patterns and distinctive temple borders. The cotton is woven with a characteristic stiffness that softens with washing.
- **Borders:** Block-printed or woven, with traditional motifs.
- **Material:** Cotton, often hand-spun.
- **Editorial note:** Chettinad sarees are less known than Kanchipuram but have a devoted following. They represent an opportunity for HOP to champion an underappreciated craft.
