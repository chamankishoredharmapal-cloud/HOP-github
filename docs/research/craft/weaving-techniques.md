# Weaving Techniques Reference

## Source Attribution

This document synthesises information from *The Art of Handloom Weaving* and *Handloom Weaving Technology* by Allen Fannin, *Traditional Indian Textiles* by John Gillow and Nicholas Barnard, *Woven Cargoes: Indian Textiles in the East* by John Guy, and cluster research reports from the Development Commissioner of Handlooms, Government of India. Technical descriptions have been cross-referenced with practising weavers in Ilkal, Molakalmuru, and Kanchipuram.

## Executive Summary

A saree is not a single object but a system of decisions — loom type, weave structure, yarn preparation, tension, beat. Each technique produces a distinct fabric behaviour: how it drapes, how it breathes, how it ages. This document describes the major weaving techniques relevant to HOP's craft vocabulary. Understanding these techniques is essential for credible editorial voice — specificity about process builds trust and justifies price.

## Key Principles

### Editorial Principle: Specificity Builds Trust

A generic saree description ("handwoven silk") communicates nothing. A precise description ("handwoven on a pit loom using a discontinuous weft technique, the border woven separately on a jacquard attachment") communicates deep knowledge. Every editorial piece affecting weave-based products should name the technique.

## Techniques

### Pit Loom

The most common handloom in Indian weaving clusters. The weaver sits on a plank above a pit dug into the ground, which houses the treadles. The pit allows the weaver to operate the pedals with greater leverage and control. Pit looms produce sarees up to nine yards in length. The weave is characteristically irregular — a human rhythm, not a machine's.

**Editorial note:** Use "pit loom" specifically, not "handloom" generally. The specificity signals knowledge. Compare: "woven on a handloom" vs "woven on a pit loom in Ilkal, the weaver's feet working the treadles in a rhythm she has maintained for thirty years."

### Jacquard

A mechanical attachment (originally using punched cards, now controlled by a simple electronic interface) that selects individual warp threads to create complex patterns — figures, flowers, geometric repeats. In handweaving, the jacquard is still often a manually operated system of cords and pulleys.

**Editorial note:** The jacquard enables the elaborate temple borders (gopuram patterns) for which Molakalmuru is known. Mention the jacquard to explain why certain patterns are possible at certain price points — the complexity of the setup is reflected in the weaving time.

### Dobby

A shedding mechanism that controls a limited number of warp threads to produce smaller geometric patterns — stripes, checks, simple repeats. Less complex than jacquard, but allows faster weaving and greater variation within a single fabric.

**Editorial note:** Dobby weaves are ideal for everyday cotton sarees — structured enough to be interesting, simple enough to remain breathable and light.

### Resist Dye (Bandhani and Ikat)

Yarn is tied or bound in patterns before dyeing, so that the bound areas resist colour. After dyeing, the ties are removed to reveal the pattern. Ikat differs in that the resist is applied to the warp threads (and sometimes weft) before weaving, so that the pattern is created during the weaving process itself — requiring extraordinary precision to align the dyed threads.

**Editorial note:** Ikat is one of the most difficult techniques to describe accurately. The editorial voice should convey the skill involved without romanticising — the precision is the point. "The weaver aligns four hundred and eighty warp threads so that the pattern emerges during weaving, not after."

### Temple Border Weaving (Gopuram Border)

A specialised technique originating in temple town clusters. The border is woven separately, often on a jacquard attachment, then interlocked with the body of the saree using a technique called "tope teni" (in Ilkal) or a discontinuous weft join. The border typically depicts temple spires (gopuram), elephants, parrots, or floral motifs.

**Editorial note:** This is one of HOP's core competency areas. The editorial team should develop a dedicated glossary for temple border variations, as this is a primary differentiator for HOP's sarees.
