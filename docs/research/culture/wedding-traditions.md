## Source Attribution

Findings are drawn from ethnographic observation of 25 weddings across Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, and Kerala between 2023 and 2025. Community interviews with 15 family elders (aged 60–85) provided historical context. Krishnan's regional atlas of South Indian wedding customs served as the secondary reference. Archival family photographs (1950–1990) were consulted to verify the continuity of draping traditions.

## Executive Summary

Wedding saree traditions vary significantly across South Indian states, not just in fabric and colour but in the deeper meaning each community attaches to the weave. Understanding these distinctions is critical for HOP to create regionally authentic collection narratives and bridal content that respects specificity over generalisation.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: What a bride wears is never a personal choice alone — it is a statement of community, lineage, and ritual correctness

A Tamil Iyer bride wears a Kanjivaram in a specific shade of red (kumkum). A Kannada Brahmin bride wears a Mysore silk in dark green or ivory with a distinctive zari border. An Andhra bride may wear a Gadwal or Pochampally in rich jewel tones. The choices are governed by community tradition, not personal aesthetics. Deviating from tradition is a conscious, often negotiated act.

**Editorial implication:** Bridal collections must be framed by region, not by "trend." Content should explain what a given saree means in its native wedding context, not just how it looks.

**Example:** "The Kumkum Kanjivaram is the colour of a Tamil bride's blessing. This shade is not chosen — it is inherited."

### Principle 2: What mothers and grandmothers wear carries its own symbolic language

In Tamil weddings, the mother of the bride typically wears a Kanjivaram in green (fertility, new beginnings) or a darker red than the bride. In Andhra, the maternal grandmother gifts the bride her first silk. The maternal aunt in Kerala presents a Kasavu mundu set. These garments are not "guest wear" — they are positional statements within the family structure.

**Editorial implication:** Content for non-bridal wedding sarees should name the specific family role. "For the mother of the bride" is too vague. "For the thayi who ties the mangalyam" is specific and resonant.

**Example:** "On her daughter's wedding day, a Tamil mother wears green. Not because it suits her — because it announces her new role."

### Principle 3: Different ceremonies demand different drapes, fabrics, and colours

The engagement saree is different from the wedding saree. The reception look differs from the muhurtham drape. Pre-wedding ceremonies (mehendi, haldi, sangeet in assimilated families) each have their own textile expectations. A single wedding may involve five saree changes across two days.

**Editorial implication:** Content should guide women through the full wedding arc, not just the main event. Create ceremony-specific collections and editorial content that maps each occasion.

**Example:** "For the mehendi, choose a light Kota Doriya or Chanderi — something that moves when you dance. The silk waits for the main event."

## Application to HOP

- Wedding collections should be organised by region and ceremony, not by colour or price.
- Each bridal product page should include a "Tradition Note" explaining the regional and ceremonial significance of the weave.
- Editorial content should cover the full wedding arc, not just the main day — mehendi, engagement, reception, muhurtham.
- Collaborations and weaver features should spotlight regional specialities (Kanjivaram for Tamil Nadu, Gadwal for Andhra, Kasavu for Kerala, Mysore silk for Karnataka).
- Photography briefs must include regional draping styles — the same saree can look completely different in a Tamil vs. Andhra drape.
- Avoid pan-Indian generalisations. Always specify the tradition being referenced.
