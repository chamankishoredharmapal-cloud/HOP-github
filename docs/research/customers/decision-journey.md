## Source Attribution

This journey map was developed through a cross-functional workshop with HOP's editorial and CX teams in March 2026. The stages were validated against 18 customer interviews (April–June 2026) and 200 session recordings (Q1 2026). The framework draws from McKinsey's updated consumer decision journey model. Post-purchase survey data (n=104, May 2026) informed the loyalty and advocacy stages.

## Executive Summary

The premium saree purchase is rarely linear. Customers enter and exit the journey multiple times, often spending weeks or months between stages. HOP's role is not to push the customer through the funnel but to be present, consistent, and trustworthy at every point she chooses to return. The journey has six stages, each with distinct needs and HOP actions.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: The journey is a cycle, not a funnel

The traditional marketing funnel assumes linear progression from awareness to purchase. The premium saree buyer loops: she discovers HOP, leaves, returns through a friend's recommendation, leaves again, and finally purchases when an occasion crystallises her need. Post-purchase, she may re-enter the cycle as a gift giver or an advocate. The model is circular, not one-directional.

**Editorial implication:** Create content that is useful at every stage, knowing the customer may jump between stages. Do not assume a first-time visitor is at "awareness." She may have heard of HOP from a friend and be in evaluation mode already.

**Example:** A first-time visitor who arrives via a friend's Instagram share may go straight to the product page, not the about page. She needs product detail as much as an editorial introduction — serve both simultaneously on the product page.

### Principle 2: Each stage has a dominant emotional need

- **Discovery:** Curiosity, delight, aesthetic appreciation
- **Exploration:** Interest, learning, comparison
- **Evaluation:** Trust, reassurance, justification
- **Purchase:** Confidence, ease, satisfaction
- **Post-Purchase:** Validation, pride, care
- **Advocacy:** Belonging, generosity, identity

Content and experience design must match the emotional need of the stage. A customer in evaluation does not need beautiful photography — she needs transparent pricing and clear policies. A customer in post-purchase does not need more product recommendations — she needs a care guide.

**Editorial implication:** Map every content asset and UX element to a journey stage. Audit regularly: what are we showing customers in evaluation? If the answer is "lifestyle photography," we are failing their emotional need.

**Example:** The post-purchase email sequence should include a saree care guide, not a "you might also like" recommendation. The customer's emotional need at this stage is validation and care, not more shopping.

## HOP Actions by Stage

| Stage | Customer Need | HOP Response |
|-------|---------------|--------------|
| **Discovery** | Beauty, curiosity, a sense of finding something rare | Editorial-first landing page. Instagram as a magazine. Word-of-mouth through remarkable packaging. |
| **Exploration** | Deep information, weave stories, weaver profiles, comparison | Rich product pages (400+ words). "Read the Story" toggle. Side-by-side compare on materials. Blog with weave guides. |
| **Evaluation** | Trust signals: transparent pricing, clear policies, real photos, authentic reviews | Pricing breakdown by cost component. Weaver attribution. Unretouched photography. No curated reviews — real customer photos. |
| **Purchase** | Frictionless checkout, dignity, no surprises | Minimal checkout (name, address, payment). No account required. No pop-ups. Transparent shipping timeline. |
| **Post-Purchase** | Pride in purchase, care knowledge, feeling remembered | Care guide in package. Personalised email from editorial team. Order tracking with human language, not just status codes. |
| **Advocacy** | Identity expression, generosity, community | Referral programme as "share the craft" (not a discount). Customer stories published as editorial. Private community for repeat buyers. |

## Application to HOP

- The website's information architecture must support non-linear browsing: editorial connects to product, product connects to weaver, weaver connects to care. The customer will take any path.
- Analytics should track movement between stages, not just conversion. A customer moving from Discovery to Exploration is a success. A customer moving from Evaluation back to Discovery (leaving out of distrust) is a problem.
- Customer service should be trained to identify which stage a customer is in based on their query and respond accordingly. An evaluation-stage query ("Is this real silk?") needs a different answer than a purchase-stage query ("Can I get it by Friday?").
- Content calendar should produce assets targeted at each stage — not just "top of funnel" and "bottom of funnel."
