## Source Attribution

This persona is constructed from 18 in-depth interviews with HOP customers conducted between April and June 2026, supplemented by an internal survey of 342 respondents (March 2026). Session recording analysis of 200 anonymised browsing sessions (Q1 2026) provided behavioural data. Desai's Bain & Co. study on the Indian premium woman consumer served as the demographic and psychographic benchmark. Competitive analysis of premium saree buyer behaviour was conducted in 2025.

## Executive Summary

The primary HOP customer is an urban Indian woman, 28–55, who values craft over fashion, buys less but chooses better. She is educated, reads widely, travels, and has opinions on fabric. She does not need to be convinced that handloom is valuable — she already knows. What she needs is a brand that matches her discernment and treats her purchase as a considered choice, not a transaction.

## Detailed Narrative

Her name might be Anjali, Meera, or Sunita. She is a lawyer in Bombay High Court, a design professor at NID, a startup founder in Bengaluru, or a senior editor in Delhi. She earns well but does not spend carelessly. Her wardrobe is curated, not accumulated. She owns 15–20 sarees, each with a story, each purchased for a specific occasion.

She reads. Long-form journalism, literary fiction, essays on culture. She subscribes to at least one publication. She has a Libby or Audible account. She can name her favourite authors. She reads about food, architecture, textiles. She is intellectually curious but not performatively so.

She travels — sometimes for work, sometimes for herself. She has been to a handloom cluster at least once. She might have visited a weaver's home. She buys souvenirs that are fabric, not trinkets.

She has strong opinions about fabric. She can tell the difference between pure silk and blended silk by touch. She knows what Kota Doriya feels like and why Chanderi is different from Maheshwari. She does not flaunt this knowledge. It is simply what she knows.

She is not impressed by discounts. A discount signals that the product was overpriced to begin with. She would rather pay full price for something made well than buy two mediocre things on sale. She does not trust brands that "constantly have offers." It feels desperate.

She is on Instagram but does not scroll endlessly. She follows 3–4 brands, 2–3 weavers, 1–2 saree enthusiasts. She engages meaningfully — comments, saves, shares with friends. She does not buy from Instagram ads. She goes to the website, reads the about page, checks the sizing, reads the care instructions, then leaves. She comes back twice or thrice before purchasing.

When she purchases, she expects:
- The saree to look exactly like the photograph (or better)
- The packaging to respect the garment
- The transaction to be dignified and simple
- The brand to be reachable if something is wrong
- The saree to last

She will return a saree that does not meet these expectations. She will not make a scene. She will simply not come back. And she will tell 2–3 friends privately.

Her loyalty, once earned, is formidable. She will become a repeat buyer. She will gift HOP sarees to her sister, her mother, her closest friend. She will defend the brand in conversation. She expects the brand to stay true to what it promised.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: She buys less but chooses better

Her wardrobe is the result of deliberate curation, not accumulation. Each saree has a purpose and a memory. She will spend 30–60 minutes reading a single product page before deciding. She will compare three sarees across two tabs. She will leave and come back. The purchase is a decision, not an impulse.

**Editorial implication:** Create content that rewards deep reading. Detailed weave descriptions, weaver profiles, and care guides. Do not simplify. She is not looking for a quick decision — she is looking for a considered one.

**Example:** A product page with a 400-word weave story, a weaver profile, and a 6-image gallery performs better than one with a price and a discount badge.

### Principle 2: She values craft over fashion

She does not care what is trending. She cares about technique, material, provenance. She wants to know who wove this saree and what makes their work distinctive. She is interested in the limitations of the medium — why a pit loom produces a certain texture, why natural dyes behave differently.

**Editorial implication:** Lead with the craft, not the occasion. Let the occasion be implied. Write about the weave first, the colour second, the styling third.

**Example:** Not "This red Kanjivaram is perfect for Diwali." But "This Kanjivaram uses a 40-year-old zari technique from a family of weavers who have been working with gold thread since 1947. The red comes from lac — a natural dye that deepens with age."

## Application to HOP

- Product pages must offer 5–10x more detail than a typical e-commerce site. Assume the customer wants to read.
- Photography should be documentary in style — showing the saree in natural light, on the loom, folded, draped, stored.
- Customer service should be staffed by people who understand the product. The customer knows more than the average customer service agent. Match her knowledge.
- Never use countdown timers, stock alerts, or discount pop-ups. She finds them insulting.
- Loyalty should be recognised thoughtfully — early access to new collections, personal notes from the editorial team — never through points or gamification.
