## Source Attribution

Twelve customer interviews focused specifically on gift purchase behaviour were conducted in May 2026. Internal purchase order analysis (2024–2026) was used to differentiate gift orders from self-orders by shipping address patterns and order notes. Sharma's study of the urban Indian gift economy provided the sociological framework. A targeted survey on gifting behaviour (n=156) was fielded in April 2026.

## Executive Summary

The intentional gift giver purchases a HOP saree not as a convenience but as a statement of regard. The saree is chosen to carry emotional weight — it must communicate thoughtfulness, taste, and understanding of the recipient. For HOP, the gift purchase is a higher-stakes transaction than a self-purchase, with deeper brand implications.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: The gift giver is buying emotional impact, not fabric

The gift giver is purchasing the feeling the recipient will have when opening the package. They are buying the weight of the box, the quality of the tissue paper, the message on the card. The saree itself is the primary object, but the ritual of giving is what matters most. If the packaging disappoints, the saree's quality becomes irrelevant to the gift experience.

**Editorial implication:** All content related to gifting should address the emotional arc of giving, not the technical specifications of the saree. Emphasise the sensory experience of receiving: the heft of the parcel, the reveal, the first touch.

**Example:** "A saree is a gift you do not open quickly. The weight of the parcel tells you something precious is inside. The fold of the tissue paper, the card handwritten, the silk that catches the light slowly."

### Principle 2: The gift giver needs help choosing — but not obvious help

The intentional gift giver knows the recipient's taste generally (she wears sarees, she prefers silks, she likes deep colours) but may not know specifics. They are looking for subtle guidance: "This saree is for someone who values tradition with a modern edge." They do not want a quiz or a gift finder tool. They want editorial that helps them infer the right choice.

**Editorial implication:** Product descriptions should naturally signal the kind of recipient the saree suits. Do not tag recipients explicitly. Instead, let the tone, the weave story, and the styling suggest the personality.

**Example:** Not "Perfect for your mother-in-law." But "This Kanjivaram has the weight of tradition and the lightness of a woman who wears it on her own terms."

## Application to HOP

- Gift packaging must be exceptional: heavy box, acid-free tissue paper, handwritten card option, cotton ribbon.
- Gift orders should ship with a "Gift Note" field prominently placed in the checkout flow.
- Editorial content should include "Giving as a Gift" sections with advice on choosing for someone else.
- The brand should avoid explicit "gift guides" in the conventional e-commerce sense. Instead, publish essays: "What It Means to Give a Saree."
- Gift receipts should be separate from the package. No pricing on any material inside the gift box.
- Returns for gift orders should be handled with exceptional discretion. The giver should never know if the recipient returned the saree.
