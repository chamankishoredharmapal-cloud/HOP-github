## Source Attribution

This typology extends the Restrained principle from editorial-principles.md and voice-bible.md into a three-part framework. Cross-industry references were selected from brands and practitioners whose work embodies restraint as a signal of confidence rather than limitation. The framework was tested against HOP's existing editorial archive (29 articles scored for each of the three restraint types).

## Executive Summary

Restraint at House of Padmavati is not minimalism. Minimalism removes for the sake of aesthetic purity. Restraint removes for the sake of meaning. This document defines three distinct types of restraint — visual, verbal, and structural — each with its own editorial application. Restraint signals confidence: a brand that can say less, show less, and offer less communicates that what it does offer is worthy of full attention.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: Visual Restraint — what you do not show

Visual restraint is the discipline of omission in imagery and layout. It means choosing one image where two could exist, leaving margins wide, and resisting the impulse to fill empty space. The most powerful visual element on a HOP page is often the one that is not there.

- **Editorial implication:** Articles must not exceed one image per 200 words. Collections must not show more than one angle of a saree per page — the detail shot and the lifestyle shot belong on separate pages or as toggleable layers, not competing for the same viewport.
- **Example:** A weaver profile uses a single image — a portrait of the weaver's hands resting on the loom. No second image of the weaver's face, no product shot, no environment shot. The hands tell the story alone.
- **Cross-industry reference:** Apple product pages show one device per viewport. The white space around the image is not empty — it is the device room to exist.

### Principle 2: Verbal Restraint — what you do not say

Verbal restraint is the discipline of word count, word choice, and structural silence. It means saying something once and trusting the reader, never qualifying or over-explaining, and leaving a sentence when it is finished rather than when every possible meaning has been extracted.

- **Editorial implication:** Every paragraph must pass the single-idea test: if it contains two ideas, split it. Every adjective must pass the necessity test: if the noun does the work alone, remove the adjective. Every sentence must pass the confidence test: if it reads like a defence or a justification, rewrite it as a statement.
- **Example:** "This saree is woven in Ilkal, Karnataka. The border is finished before the body begins. The two are joined by a knot that has held for four centuries." 26 words. No adjectives. Each sentence a complete thought. The reader is trusted to feel the weight.
- **Cross-industry reference:** The Row's runway notes are frequently two sentences. Muji product descriptions list material and dimensions — nothing more.

### Principle 3: Structural Restraint — what you do not include

Structural restraint is the discipline of feature selection and content architecture. It means choosing not to include a feature not because it is bad but because including it would dilute what already exists. It means a navigation with five items when ten could be justified. It means a collection with twelve sarees when forty are available.

- **Editorial implication:** Every content unit — article, collection, page — is audited for structural restraint before launch. Any element that exists "because every site has one" is removed. The question is never "could a reader want this?" but "does this make what we have stronger?"
- **Example:** HOP's website has no size guide (every saree is one-size, and the drape accommodates all bodies — a size guide would imply standardised fit where none exists), no wishlist (saving for later contradicts the philosophy that when you find a handwoven saree, the time is now), and no "customer also bought" section (each saree is singular — pairing it with others commoditises it).
- **Cross-industry reference:** Dieter Rams' 10th principle: "Good design is as little design as possible." Muji's product development process includes a "what if we don't include this" stage. Kiarostami's cinema relies on what happens off-screen.

## Application to HOP

Restraint is audited at three checkpoints: editorial brief (structural — does this content unit earn its existence?), first draft (verbal — every adjective and sentence tested), and pre-publish layout review (visual — image density and white space measured). The three-type framework is used as a shared vocabulary between Editorial and Design teams. When a disagreement arises about whether to include or remove an element, the teams ask: "Which type of restraint does this violate?"
