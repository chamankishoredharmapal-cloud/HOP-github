# Choice Architecture — Curation as Cognitive Relief

## Source Attribution

This document synthesises research on choice overload (Iyengar & Lepper, 2000), the paradox of choice (Schwartz, 2004), and the compromise effect (Simonson, 1989), as well as Thaler and Sunstein's work on choice architecture in *Nudge* (2008). The application to curated retail and editorial design is original to House of Padmavati.

## Executive Summary

When faced with too many options, the human brain experiences decision fatigue and analysis paralysis, leading to purchase abandonment or post-purchase dissatisfaction. For a premium, high-involvement product like a handwoven saree, the risk of choice overload is especially acute. HOP mitigates this through curated selection — small collections, intentional design, and a clear path from discovery to purchase. Curation is not a limitation; it is cognitive relief.

## Key Principles

### 1. Curated Discovery

**Definition:** When a customer encounters a large set of options, her brain must engage in extensive comparison, increasing cognitive load and reducing the likelihood of any purchase. Curated discovery — presenting a small, intentional set of options — reduces cognitive load and increases purchase confidence. The curator's judgment substitutes for the customer's exhaustive search.

**Editorial implication:** Collections must be small and intentional. Each collection should have a clear identity, a specific emotional territory, and a limited number of products. The editorial voice should present each collection as a considered set, not as a category filter. The reader should feel that the curation has already done the work of selection for her.

**Example:** "The Kalyani collection holds five sarees. Each one belongs to a winter wedding where the light fades by five. Each one is woven in Molakalmuru silk, with a temple border that has been passed through four generations." (The curation is explicit — five sarees, one purpose, one region.)

### 2. Progressive Commitment

**Definition:** Large decisions feel risky. Progressive commitment breaks a single high-stakes decision into a series of smaller, lower-stakes decisions, each building on the last. The customer moves from browsing a collection to viewing a product to reading its story to adding it to the cart — each step is a small commitment that makes the next step easier.

**Editorial implication:** The customer journey must be designed as a series of progressive commitments, not a single leap from discovery to purchase. Each page should advance the reader one step further: from collection (curiosity) to product (desire) to specifications (confidence) to cart (commitment). No page should ask the reader to make the final decision before she is ready.

**Example:** The emotional journey maps this progression explicitly: Arrival → Curiosity → Wonder → Trust → Desire → Confidence → Ownership. Each stage is a page or interaction that advances the reader one step. The checkout is not the first moment of commitment — it is the last of many small commitments.

### 3. Default Selection

**Definition:** When faced with a complex decision, people tend to accept the default option — the path of least resistance. Defaults are powerful choice-architecture tools because they require no cognitive effort to select. In a premium context, the default should be the option that best serves the customer's long-term satisfaction.

**Editorial implication:** HOP should present the most intentional choice as the default. On a product page, the primary CTA should be the most considered option (e.g., "Add to Bag" rather than "Buy Now" — the former implies consideration, the latter implies impulse). On the gift page, the default should be the full gift experience (wrapping + card), not the bare product. Defaults should never be manipulative; they should reflect the house's understanding of what the customer will value most.

**Example:** The gift page defaults to "Gift Wrapping + Keepsake Card" selected, with the option to deselect. The default reflects the house's belief that a gift from HOP should be a ceremony. The customer can choose otherwise, but the default is the intentional choice.

## Application to HOP

**Content rules:**

1. **Collections must contain no more than eight products.** Beyond this number, cognitive load increases and purchase confidence decreases. If a collection exceeds this limit, it must be split into sub-collections or the curation must be tightened.

2. **Product filtering must be minimal.** Filter by collection, by occasion, or by colour family — not by eight attributes. The curation should have already done the work of filtering.

3. **The path from discovery to purchase must have no more than four steps.** Collection → Product Detail → Cart → Checkout. Every additional step increases the risk of abandonment.

4. **No "You might also like" or "Complete the look" sections.** These add choice at the moment of commitment, increasing cognitive load and risking abandonment. The reader's focus should be on the saree she has chosen, not on additional options.

5. **The cart should show only what has been added.** No recommendations, no upsells, no cross-sells. The cart is a confirmation space, not a discovery space.

6. **Defaults should be intentional.** Gift wrapping should default to selected. The primary CTA should be "Add to Bag" (considered) rather than "Buy Now" (impulsive). The checkout should default to the most convenient shipping option.
