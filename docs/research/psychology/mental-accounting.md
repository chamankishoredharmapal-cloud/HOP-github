# Mental Accounting — How Customers Categorise Spending

## Source Attribution

This document applies Richard Thaler's mental accounting theory (1985, 1999) to the premium saree purchase context. Mental accounting describes how people categorise expenditures into mental accounts (e.g., "necessities," "investments," "gifts," "luxuries") and how the framing of a purchase determines which account it enters. The application to heirloom and luxury goods draws on Prelec and Loewenstein's work on the pain of paying.

## Executive Summary

Mental accounting explains why a customer might hesitate at a ₹25,000 saree but spend ₹50,000 on a family holiday without blinking — the two purchases belong to different mental accounts. HOP's editorial task is to frame every saree purchase in the "heirloom investment" or "generational gift" account, where price is secondary to enduring value. When a saree is categorised as an investment rather than an expense, the pain of paying diminishes and satisfaction increases.

## Key Principles

### 1. Account Framing

**Definition:** The mental account a customer places a purchase in determines her willingness to pay. The same ₹25,000 feels reasonable in the "heirloom investment" account and excessive in the "clothing expense" account. Editorial framing is the mechanism that guides which account is activated.

**Editorial implication:** Every product description, collection story, and journal article must frame the saree as an enduring object — something that outlives seasons, trends, and even its first wearer. Language of investment, legacy, and continuance activates the heirloom account. Language of fashion, trend, or seasonality activates the clothing expense account and must be avoided.

**Example:** "This saree will be worn, folded, stored, and passed down. It is not a purchase for this season — it is a choice for the next generation." (Heirloom investment account.) Compare: "The perfect saree for this wedding season" (Clothing expense account — weaker framing.)

### 2. Gift Accounting

**Definition:** Gift purchases occupy a distinct mental account with different price sensitivity. When buying for someone else, the customer is more willing to spend on presentation, meaning, and emotional weight. The gift account is also less susceptible to buyer's remorse because the purchase is framed as generosity rather than self-indulgence.

**Editorial implication:** The gift section must emphasise the ceremony of giving — the jasmine paper wrapping, the handwritten keepsake card, the intention behind the choice. The product itself is part of the gift, but the framing must extend beyond the saree to the entire gifting ritual. The price should be presented as a reflection of the recipient's worth, not the giver's budget.

**Example:** "A gift from House of Padmavati is not a package — it is a ceremony. Hand-wrapped in jasmine paper, tied with cotton ribbon, accompanied by a keepsake card printed in Ink Charcoal on Jasmine Mist cardstock. Your message, hand-written. The saree, chosen with intention." (Gift accounting — the price is part of the ceremony, not the focus.)

### 3. Premium Justification

**Definition:** The premium price of a handwoven saree must be justified within the mental account the customer has chosen. If the account is "heirloom investment," the justification is durability, generational quality, and timeless design. If the account is "craft appreciation," the justification is the hours of labour, the skill of the weaver, and the authenticity of the materials.

**Editorial implication:** Never justify price directly ("this is worth every rupee"). Instead, provide the evidence that allows the customer to justify the price to herself. The editorial role is to supply the facts that support the mental account she has chosen. Different customers may use different accounts; the content must support multiple framings.

**Example:** "Twenty-one days on the loom. Pure mulberry silk, hand-spun zari. A weave that grows softer with each wearing." (Supports both the "craft appreciation" account and the "heirloom investment" account.)

## Application to HOP

**Content rules:**

1. **Never use the word "investment" directly.** The framing must be implicit. Show the qualities that make the saree an investment — durability, generational quality, timeless design — and let the customer draw the conclusion.

2. **Gift content must describe the ceremony, not the transaction.** The gift page should lead with the wrapping ritual, the keepsake card, the intention. Price should be presented as a secondary detail, not a primary feature.

3. **Product descriptions must include at least one generational frame.** A sentence that connects the saree to the idea of passing it down, of it outliving its first wearer, of it being folded and stored and remembered. This activates the heirloom account.

4. **Avoid seasonal or trend-based language.** "This season's must-have" activates the clothing expense account. "A weave that belongs to winter evenings" activates the heirloom account through specificity of time and place.

5. **The Continuance messaging pillar is the primary vehicle for mental accounting.** Every piece of content that serves the Continuance pillar is, by definition, framing the saree in the heirloom investment account.
