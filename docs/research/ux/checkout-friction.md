## Source Attribution

Abandonment analysis was conducted on 4,872 shopping carts created on the HOP website between 2025 and 2026. Kapoor's UX Design Review study on premium retail checkout provided the framework. Session recordings of 100 checkout sessions (Q1 2026) revealed specific friction points. A competitive audit of 10 premium Indian fashion brands' checkout flows was conducted in 2025. A customer survey on checkout preferences (n=156) was fielded in April 2026.

## Executive Summary

Checkout friction is the single fastest way to lose a premium customer who has already decided to purchase. The premium buyer's tolerance for friction is lower than the mass-market buyer's — she will abandon a cart over a hidden cost, a forced account creation, or a confusing form. HOP's checkout must be the quietest, most dignified transaction in Indian e-commerce.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: Premium checkout must feel like a concierge service, not a form

The standard e-commerce checkout asks the customer to do the work: create an account, fill 10 fields, accept terms, find the coupon code box, navigate through 4 steps. The premium customer has already done the hard work — she decided to buy. Everything after that is HOP's job to make effortless. The ideal checkout has the fewest possible fields, no unnecessary decisions, and no surprises.

**Editorial implication:** Checkout copy should be warm and minimal. Form labels should be human: "Where should we send it?" not "Shipping Address *." Buttons should say "Complete Your Order" not "Submit."

**Example:** The entire checkout flow should be: 1) Email address, 2) Shipping address (auto-detect city from pincode), 3) Payment. No optional fields. No account creation requirement. No "Would you like to add a gift note?" — ask that on the product page, not during checkout.

### Principle 2: Hidden costs are a betrayal of trust

The most common cause of abandonment in premium e-commerce is unexpected costs appearing at the final step — shipping, taxes, duties. For a premium customer, this is not just a friction point. It is a breach of trust. She feels misled. The cost presented on the product page must be the full cost of acquiring the saree.

**Editorial implication:** Display all-in pricing on the product page. If shipping is free, say "Free shipping" clearly. If there are taxes, include them in the displayed price. No surprises at checkout.

**Example:** "₹18,500 — inclusive of all taxes. Free shipping. 15-day returns." This single line at the product page price point eliminates the most common abandonment trigger.

## Application to HOP

- Checkout must be a single-page flow, not a multi-step process. All information on one scrollable page.
- Guest checkout always available. Account creation offered as an option after purchase, never during.
- No coupon code field. HOP does not do discounts. If a promotion is offered (rarely), apply it automatically or use a direct link.
- Payment options: UPI, net banking, credit/debit card, and one-click payment (saved card/UPI for returning customers). Avoid EMI offers — they feel like the customer cannot afford the purchase.
- Shipping timeline displayed before payment, not after. "Delivered by July 30" is better than "5–7 business days."
- Order confirmation page should feel like the end of a good transaction: "Your saree is being carefully packed. We will send you a note when it ships."
- Post-purchase: automated email within 2 minutes of order confirmation, with clear next steps. Not a marketing email — a confirmation.
