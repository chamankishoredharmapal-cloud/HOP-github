## Source Attribution

Mobile session analytics from 12,450 mobile sessions on the HOP website (2025–2026) provided the quantitative foundation. Park's longitudinal study on mobile reading behaviour (2025) supplied the theoretical framework. Session recordings of 200 mobile sessions (Q1 2026) gave qualitative insight into touch-based navigation and content interaction. Social media referral analysis tracked HOP Instagram traffic patterns (2025–2026). A/B test results from content layout experiments (February 2026) validated formatting decisions.

## Executive Summary

Mobile content consumption at HOP differs significantly from desktop: sessions are shorter, scrolling is faster, and the willingness to read long-form content is lower — but not absent. Mobile users demand instant sensory reward within the first screenful. If the opening does not captivate, the mobile user swipes away within 3–5 seconds.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: Mobile readers need instant sensory reward

On desktop, a reader may give a piece 10–15 seconds to prove itself. On mobile, the window is 3–5 seconds. The headline and first paragraph must be immediately gratifying. The reader's thumb is poised to scroll — or to leave. The opening must deliver texture, emotion, or a moment that feels worth the thumb's effort.

**Editorial implication:** Write mobile-first openings, even if the content is also published on desktop. The opening paragraph should be shorter on mobile (1–2 sentences) and should land the central image or idea immediately.

**Example:** Desktop opening: "In the weaving clusters of Kanchipuram, a tradition that spans centuries continues in the homes of families who have known nothing else." Mobile opening: "Her grandmother's hands taught her the zari. Now she teaches her daughter."

### Principle 2: Scrolling is faster, so breaks must be sharper

Mobile users scroll more quickly and with less precision. They do not "pause" at natural breaks the way desktop readers do. Each paragraph must be self-contained enough to catch the eye during a fast scroll. Subheadings, image breaks, and short paragraphs act as visual anchors that slow the scroll and invite reading.

**Editorial implication:** On mobile, no paragraph should exceed 3 sentences. Insert an image or pull quote every 150–200 words. Subheadings should be frequent and act as summaries: a mobile reader should be able to skim subheadings and understand the article's arc.

**Example:** A 600-word editorial piece should have 4–5 subheadings, at least 2 images, and no paragraph longer than 3 sentences.

## Application to HOP

- All editorial content must be designed for mobile first, then adapted for desktop. Write and format for the smaller screen.
- Headlines should be 6–8 words maximum on mobile. Desktop can handle longer headlines; mobile cannot.
- Images should be full-width on mobile and load within 1 second. Compressed WebP format with lazy loading.
- Font size: minimum 16px body text on mobile. 14px is too small for comfortable reading.
- Tap targets (links, buttons, image galleries) should be minimum 48×48px for thumb navigation.
- Social sharing buttons should be prominently placed at the end of every editorial piece, not hidden in a menu.
- The first screenful (above the fold on mobile) must contain the headline, the opening paragraph, and at least one compelling visual element.
- Avoid sidebars, multi-column layouts, and hover-reveal interactions on mobile. Stack everything vertically.
- Voice-bible tone on mobile should be slightly more direct: shorter sentences, simpler vocabulary, to match the faster reading pace.
