## Source Attribution

Eye-tracking research was conducted with 25 participants reading editorial content on the HOP website in April 2026. Nielsen's foundational research on web reading behaviour (1997, updated 2023) provided the theoretical framework. Lewis's study on sensory writing and reader engagement (2024) informed the writing psychology. Session recordings of 150 editorial content sessions (Q1 2026) validated the findings in HOP's context. A/B test results from HOP's product pages (March 2026) provided quantitative support.

## Executive Summary

HOP's target audience reads editorial content differently from typical web users: they read more deeply and are more willing to scroll, but they still follow the F-pattern initially before committing. The key is to honour both patterns — satisfy the scanner with clear structure, then reward the committed reader with sensory depth.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: The F-pattern governs first engagement, but depth follows trust

Users initially scan content in an F-shaped pattern: they read the first few lines fully, then skim down the left side, then scan horizontally at natural breakpoints. However, HOP's audience diverges from the norm: if the opening is sufficiently compelling, they will switch from scanning to deep reading. The transition happens within the first 50–80 words. The opening must earn the right to be read deeply.

**Editorial implication:** Write openings that are sensory and specific. Avoid generic introductions. Lead with texture, colour, or a moment. The first sentence must reward the scanner and hook the reader.

**Example:** Weak opening: "The Kanjivaram silk saree has a long history in Tamil Nadu." Strong opening: "The first thing you notice about a Kanjivaram is the weight. Not heavy — substantial."

### Principle 2: Short paragraphs maintain momentum

Long paragraphs lose readers. On screens, paragraphs longer than 4–5 lines cause drop-off. Each paragraph should be a single idea expressed in 2–4 sentences. White space is not wasted space — it is invitation space. It tells the reader that the next thought is worth pausing for.

**Editorial implication:** Enforce a strict paragraph length limit. Every paragraph must be editable to under 5 lines. Use single-sentence paragraphs for emphasis, but sparingly — they lose impact if overused.

**Example:** A 200-word block on zari technique should be broken into 3–4 short paragraphs, each covering one aspect: what zari is, how it is made, why it matters, how to care for it.

## Application to HOP

- All editorial content must pass the "F-pattern test": the first 80 words should be scannable and contain the core thesis.
- Paragraphs should average 2–4 sentences. Maximum 5 lines per paragraph on a standard desktop viewport.
- Headers should be meaningful and sensory, not descriptive. Not "Introduction to Kanjivaram" but "The Weight of Tradition."
- Use bulleted or numbered lists for process content (care guides, identification guides) but not for narrative editorial.
- Pull quotes should be used to break up long editorial pieces, but only for genuinely quotable lines — not for filler.
- The first image on an editorial page should appear within the first 200 words, not below a full screen of text.
- Mobile paragraphs should be shorter than desktop paragraphs — 2–3 sentence maximum on mobile.
