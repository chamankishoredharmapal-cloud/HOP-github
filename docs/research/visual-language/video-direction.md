## Source Attribution

This direction synthesises an internal review of HOP's existing video library, a cinematic reference study of three directors whose visual language embodies slowness and naturalism, a competitive audit of artisan brand video content on Instagram and YouTube, and a structured customer survey conducted in June 2026 to understand which video qualities drive craft comprehension and purchase intent.

## Executive Summary

House of Padmavati video embraces the slow gaze. Every video is a study in patience — the camera holds, the scene breathes, the fabric speaks. There is no background music, no voiceover, no jump cuts. The loom is a character, the hands of the weaver are the protagonists, and the saree in motion — draping, walking, light catching the zari — is the narrative. If a video can be understood on mute, it has succeeded.

## Key Principles

### Principle 1: Silence is the soundtrack

No background music. No voiceover. The audio track consists entirely of natural sound — the clack of the shuttle, the rustle of silk, footsteps on a stone floor, the distant sound of a kitchen, the wind through an open window. Silence between sounds is not dead air; it is the viewer's space to notice.

- **Editorial implication:** Audio mixing must treat natural sounds as primary elements. Silence is not to be filled. If a scene has 30 seconds of near-silence with only the soft sound of fabric moving, that silence stays.
- **Example:** A 90-second video of a weaver at work. The first 20 seconds: only the sound of the loom, a steady rhythmic clack. The camera does not move. The viewer's attention narrows to the weaver's hands and the fabric growing under them.

### Principle 2: The loom is a character

The weaving process is not contextual — it is central. The loom must appear in every collection video. A saree that is only shown draped, without showing the loom that made it, is incomplete.

- **Editorial implication:** Every collection video must include at least one shot of the loom. This can be a wide shot of a weaver at work, a close-up of the shuttle passing through the warp, or a static shot of a resting loom in natural light.
- **Example:** A video introducing the Coastal Bloom collection opens with a 15-second static frame of an empty loom in morning light. The camera holds. Then a hand enters the frame, picks up the shuttle, and begins. The narrative starts with the tool, not the product.

### Principle 3: Hands and fabric are the protagonists

Faces are secondary. The primary subjects are hands (weaving, draping, folding, touching) and fabric (falling, catching light, being held). When a face appears, it should feel like a gift, not a requirement.

- **Editorial implication:** Video briefs must specify hand-and-fabric shots as the priority. Portrait shots are limited to one per video and should last no longer than 5 seconds unless the expression carries significant narrative weight.
- **Example:** A saree-care video shows a woman's hands folding a Chanderi saree, layer by layer, on a wooden bed. The camera follows the hands. Her face appears once, briefly, as she looks down at the fabric with quiet attention. Then the hands continue.

## Application to HOP

Video is deployed in three formats: long-form (90–180s, YouTube and Journal — weaver stories, collection introductions), medium-form (30–60s, Instagram and website — saree in motion, detail studies), and short-form (15s, Instagram Reels, no music — the saree catching light). All formats follow the same principles: no music, no voiceover, natural sound, hands and fabric as protagonists, loom as character. A pre-production checklist enforces lighting, sound, and subject requirements before a shoot is approved.

## Sources

- HOP video archive — 12 videos reviewed. Weakness: inconsistent pacing, occasional background music that contradicted the brand's quietness. 5 June 2026.
- Cinematic references — Chantal Akerman (Jeanne Dielman — domestic duration and silence), Apichatpong Weerasethakul (Uncle Boonmee — stillness and natural sound), Souleymane Cissé (Yeelen — fabric as narrative element).
- Social video benchmarks — 8 artisan brands including Botter (slow fashion video), The Fabindia video library, and three Indian handloom Instagram accounts with >50k followers. 10–20 June 2026.
- Customer video survey — n=18, June 2026. 83% of respondents said videos without music felt more "trustworthy" and "serious." 72% said they would watch longer videos (90s+) if the content was craft-focused.
