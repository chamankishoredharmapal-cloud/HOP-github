The road to Molakalmuru is unpaved for the last three kilometres. The driver slows to a crawl. The dust rises in clouds that hang in the still morning air. By the time we arrive, the village is already awake. The looms have been running since five.

---
type: body
---

Molakalmuru is a weaving cluster in eastern Karnataka, known for temple border sarees woven on pit looms. The town has perhaps three hundred looms in active use, down from a thousand twenty years ago. The younger generation has moved to Bangalore. Those who remain are in their forties and fifties. They weave the way their parents wove, on the same looms, in the same rooms, with the same windows catching the same morning light.

We spent the day with Gangamma, a fourth-generation weaver. She began at twelve. Her mother began at ten. Her grandmother began at eight. "It was different then," she said. "Every woman in the village wove. Now it is only a few of us."

type: body

The afternoon heat is oppressive. The weavers stop during the hottest hours — from one to four — and resume in the late afternoon when the light is softer. Gangamma uses the break to prepare the warp for the next saree. There is always a next saree. The work does not end.

"What will happen when you stop weaving?" we asked.

"The sarees will stop," she said. Not bitterly. Simply.

---
type: closure
---

We left at dusk. The road was quieter. The dust had settled. Somewhere behind us, a loom was still running — the rhythm carrying into the evening, the way it has for generations. A day in Molakalmuru is not remarkable. It is simply the continuation of something that began long before any of us arrived.
