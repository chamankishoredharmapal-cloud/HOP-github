---
type: intro
---

The Ilkal saree is distinguished by its tope-teni pallu — a unique feature where the pallu is woven separately and attached to the body of the saree with a visible joint. This joint is not a flaw. It is a signature. Draping an Ilkal requires understanding this signature.

---
type: body
---

Begin by tucking the plain end of the saree into the petticoat at the navel. Make one full wrap around the waist. The pleats should fall forward, crisp and even. Seven to nine pleats is traditional. More than that and the saree becomes bulky at the waist. Fewer and the drape loses its structure.

---
type: step
number: 1
---

Tuck the plain end at the navel. Wrap once around the waist. Ensure the bottom hem grazes the ankle — no shorter, no longer.

---
type: step
number: 2
---

Pleat the remaining fabric. Seven to nine pleats. Each pleat should be roughly six inches wide. Hold them together and tuck into the petticoat, slightly left of centre.

---
type: step
number: 3
---

Bring the pallu across the body, from right to left. The tope-teni joint should sit at the shoulder — not on the arm, not at the collarbone. This is the most important adjustment. If the joint sits correctly, the drape will hold. If it does not, the pallu will pull.

---
type: step
number: 4
---

Pleat the pallu loosely. The Ilkal pallu is designed to fall in soft folds — not stiff, not structured. Pin it at the shoulder. The pin should be invisible. The saree should appear to hold itself.

---
type: step
number: 5
---

Adjust the length of the pallu at the back. It should fall to the back of the knee — no shorter. The tope-teni joint should remain visible at the shoulder. This is not a detail to hide. It is the thing that makes an Ilkal an Ilkal.

---
type: closure
---

The Ilkal drape rewards patience. It is not a saree for rushing. Adjust the pleats twice. Check the pallu length three times. Walk around. Sit down. Stand up. The drape will settle. When it does, you will feel it — the cloth has found its place.
