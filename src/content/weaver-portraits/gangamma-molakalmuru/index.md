type: intro

Gangamma has been weaving since she was twelve. She is now forty-three. In those thirty-one years, she has woven the same border pattern thousands of times — and each one is different. "The thread changes with the weather," she says. "In summer, it is tighter. In monsoon, it relaxes. You adjust. The loom tells you."

---
type: body
---

Her grandmother taught her. Her mother taught her grandmother. The pattern — a temple border with geometric motifs that echo the gopuram of the local temple — has not changed in four generations. It does not need to. "Some designs arrive complete," Gangamma says. "We only continue them."

She works on a pit loom, the simplest of all handlooms. The warp stretches across the room. The weaver sits below the level of the fabric, her feet working the treadles, her hands passing the shuttle. The rhythm is hypnotic once you are used to it. Gangamma has been in this rhythm for thirty-one years.

type: body

A single saree takes twenty-one days. Not because the weaving is slow — because it is precise. The zari is hand-spun, which means every strand is slightly different. The tension must be adjusted for each one. The border is woven separately from the body, then attached. Each section requires a different setup.

"What do you think about while you weave?" we asked her.

She laughed. "Nothing. Everything. The same thing you think about when you breathe."

She does not know where her sarees go. She knows they are worn by women in cities — Bombay, Bangalore, maybe further. She does not need to know them. "The saree carries what it needs to carry," she says. "My part is the weave. Their part is the wearing. Both are enough."
